Moon Base Progress Tracker

Trackspace is a simple, high-signal app for understanding NASA's path from Artemis missions to a sustained lunar base. The product should explain capabilities, dependencies, milestones, and evidence, not just list space news.

Principle Screens Command Center Diagrams Data Model Accuracy Sources Workflow MVP Next Steps

Project Goal

The app should answer one main question: how close is NASA to sustaining people and infrastructure on the Moon? It should keep the experience minimal: four primary screens, one reusable detail drawer, and a source-first data model.

Artemis missions are major milestones, not the outer boundary of the product. The broader frame is NASA's Moon to Mars architecture and the practical path toward long-term lunar operations.

What the app is

  • A readiness tracker for NASA's long-term lunar base plans.
  • A dependency map for the capabilities required to live and work on the Moon.
  • A spatial command layer for launch sites, landing sites, orbits, and trajectories.
  • A timeline of tests, launches, incidents, reviews, slips, and milestones.
  • A source-backed explanation tool, not a rumor board.

Event detail is not a page

Events open in a drawer from any screen. The drawer shows what happened, what is confirmed, what is unknown, affected capabilities, possible downstream impacts, and source links. This keeps the main app from growing extra pages too early.

Command Center Structure

The Command Center should be the memorable first screen: a 3D Earth/Moon scene with useful overlays. It should feel like a spatial control room, but the surrounding panels must keep the information understandable.

Ready Completed milestones and capabilities with no known blocker.
Watch Progressing, but has schedule or technical uncertainty.
Blocker Known issue that may affect the lunar base critical path.
Unknown Important item with insufficient public information.

3D simulation overlays

  • Launch sites: KSC, Cape Canaveral, Starbase, Stennis test stands, contractor sites where relevant.
  • Lunar locations: South Pole landing regions, candidate surface zones, Gateway lunar orbit context.
  • Trajectories: official mission paths when available; conceptual arcs otherwise clearly labeled.
  • Assets: SLS, Orion, Gateway, HLS landers, Blue Moon, Starship HLS, rovers, suits, power, comms.
  • Incidents: visible markers for affected pads, vehicles, test stands, and downstream dependencies.

Accuracy Policy

Trackspace should be useful because it is careful. The app can detect signals from many places, but user-facing readiness and impact labels for lunar base progress should only change when the claim has enough source support.

Default posture: official facts first, inference second, rumor never. When impact is uncertain, say "unknown" instead of inventing precision.

Claim labels

Label Meaning Allowed source basis
Confirmed A thing happened or an official schedule/status exists. NASA, FAA, OIG, GAO, partner agency, or responsible contractor statement.
Reported A reputable outlet reports an event before official details are complete. AP, Reuters, SpaceNews, Ars Technica, or similarly accountable reporting.
Inferred The app is connecting a known event to a likely dependency impact. Must reference the confirmed event plus the dependency link used for the inference.
Conceptual Used for visual paths, trajectories, or architecture that are illustrative. Clearly label when exact trajectory, ephemeris, or site data is unavailable.
Unverified Discovery signal only. It can create a private review task, not a public claim. Social media, forums, livestream clips, Wikipedia, or uncited launch trackers.

Accurate Information Sources

Use source tiers. The app can ingest many feeds, but only source-backed facts should drive readiness and impact labels.

Tier Use Starting sources Rule
Tier 1 Official Moon to Mars architecture, mission pages, launch schedule, NASA updates. NASA Moon to Mars Architecture, NASA Architecture Definition Documents, NASA Artemis, NASA Artemis V, NASA Launch Schedule, NASA Artemis Blog, NASA RSS Feeds Highest authority for mission status, dates, and architecture.
Tier 1 Major lunar architecture capabilities. NASA Human Landing Systems, NASA Starship HLS, NASA Gateway, NASA Suits and Rovers, NASA Lunar Terrain Vehicle, NASA LunaNet, NASA lunar landing regions Use for capability definitions and official relationships between capabilities.
Tier 1 Oversight, schedule risk, audits, and program management risk. NASA OIG HLS report, NASA OIG spacesuit report, GAO Artemis reports, NASA Technical Reports Server Use for risk context. Do not turn audit projections into certain outcomes.
Tier 1 Launch/reentry licensing, commercial space operations, mishap/regulatory context. FAA Commercial Space Transportation, FAA vehicle operator licenses, FAA commercial space by the numbers, FAA commercial space activities Use for regulatory status and mishap process. Avoid guessing investigation outcomes.
Tier 2 Official contractor and partner updates. Blue Origin Blue Moon, Blue Origin New Glenn, Blue Origin News, SpaceX Updates, Axiom Suit, Axiom Space News, ESA Gateway, CSA Canadarm3 Use for hardware progress, but verify lunar base impact against NASA when possible.
Tier 2 Structured launch/event feeds and media assets. Launch Library 2 API, The Space Devs LL2 overview, NASA Open APIs, NASA Image and Video Library, NASA Images API docs Use as convenience data. Cross-check key lunar base items with primary sources.
Tier 3 Reputable reporting when official sources lag behind. AP New Glenn follow-up, AP New Glenn incident report, SpaceNews Artemis search, Ars Technica Space, Reuters Connect Good for early event detection. Mark downstream impact as inference unless official confirmation exists.
Tier 4 Low-confidence discovery only. Reddit, X, YouTube clips, Wikipedia, forums, unverified trackers. Never use alone for app claims. Use only to find leads that get verified elsewhere.
For 3D trajectory accuracy, default to "conceptual" unless the app has official mission profile data, ephemeris, or NASA trajectory materials. A pretty but wrong trajectory is worse than a clearly labeled conceptual one.

Update Workflow

The earliest version can be manually curated. The important part is preserving source links and confidence rules from the beginning.

  1. Detect a new item from NASA, FAA, contractor feeds, Launch Library 2, or reputable reporting.
  2. Create or update an Event record with exact source links and publication dates.
  3. Attach affected Capability and Milestone records.
  4. Assign impact: none, watch, likely delay, blocker, or unknown.
  5. Separate confirmed facts from unknowns and inference.
  6. Show the change in Command Center, Timeline, and any affected mission or buildout phase.

Example: launchpad incident handling

Confirmed Vehicle, date, location, test type, source links, official statements.
Unknown Damage severity, investigation duration, repair timeline, alternate launch path.
Impact model Pad -> New Glenn launch cadence -> Blue Moon test schedule -> sustained lunar access readiness.
UI label Use Watch or Unknown until official impact is established.

MVP Scope

Build the smallest useful version around one lunar base outcome, one dependency graph, and a small curated event set.

First useful dataset

  • Outcome: sustained human lunar surface operations.
  • Milestones: Artemis missions, HLS demos, rover milestones, surface power demos, habitat/logistics milestones.
  • Capabilities: SLS, Orion, Gateway, Blue Moon, New Glenn, Starship HLS, spacesuits, LTV, surface power, lunar comms, habitats, logistics.
  • Locations: Kennedy Space Center, Cape Canaveral LC-36, lunar South Pole regions, Gateway lunar orbit context.
  • Events: official Artemis milestones, HLS tests, New Glenn tests/flights, Starship HLS tests, spacesuit milestones, rover milestones, regulatory reviews.
  • Interactions: rotate 3D scene, select object, open event drawer, filter timeline by milestone/capability/impact.

What to avoid early

  • Do not create ten pages. Keep four screens and one event drawer.
  • Do not claim exact delay estimates unless an authoritative source does.
  • Do not treat future dates as firm unless NASA or the responsible agency says they are firm.
  • Do not render exact-looking trajectories if the underlying data is conceptual.
  • Do not rely on social media clips or forum posts as source-backed facts.

Next Steps

The current dataset is a solid Artemis-readiness baseline: it answers "is NASA's Artemis program on track?" But the product goal is broader — how close are we to a sustained lunar base? Today's records lean heavily toward getting to the Moon (launch, crew vehicle, lander, refueling) and track a single actor on a single dimension. The next phase should widen coverage along three axes without loosening the source-first discipline.

None of this changes the accuracy policy. Every new capability, milestone, and actor must carry the same source tiers, confidence labels, and "last verified" provenance as the existing baseline. Breadth should not cost rigor.

Three axes to widen

  • Deepen the taxonomy — track living on the Moon, not just reaching it.
  • Go multi-actor — China ILRS, commercial landers, international partners, not NASA alone.
  • Add dimensions — budget, schedule slip, and risk, not readiness alone.

1. Missing capability domains (highest leverage)

The 12 capabilities today are transport-weighted. A sustained surface presence depends on a class of systems not yet tracked.

Domain Why it is essential Note
Life support / ECLSS Closed-loop air and water recycling is the line between visits and a base. Not tracked at all — the single biggest omission.
Radiation shielding Galactic cosmic rays plus solar particle events; habitat and transit both need it. A hard limiter on crew stay duration.
Lunar night survival 14 days of dark at roughly −170°C demands energy storage, not just generation. Current power tracks fission generation, not storage or thermal.
Dust mitigation Regolith is abrasive and electrostatic — the top Apollo lesson; kills seals, suits, mechanisms, and lungs. A cross-cutting risk that is currently invisible.
Crew health & medicine Bone and muscle loss, in-situ medical care, and food systems for long stays. Human-factors readiness is unmodeled.
Water / ice prospecting ISRU is meaningless until the ice is located and characterized in the polar PSRs. VIPER's status is directly relevant here.
Construction & manufacturing Landing pads, berms, regolith sintering, and 3D printing precede "sustained." Needed before the base phase is real.
Thermal control, airlocks & dust-locks Rejecting heat and keeping dust out of the habitat are daily-operations problems. Supports nearly every surface capability.

2. Broaden beyond a single actor

A serious "are we getting a lunar base" tracker is multi-program. This is the highest-payoff way to grow milestone and timeline density.

China ILRS International Lunar Research Station — Chang'e 7/8, base targeted ~2035. The direct competitor; conspicuously absent.
CLPS landers Intuitive Machines, Astrobotic, Firefly Blue Ghost. Flying now and de-risking everything downstream.
Commercial vehicles Blue Origin Blue Moon and the Starship test campaign deserve their own milestone tracks.
International partners ESA beyond ESM, plus JAXA, ISRO, and CSA contributions to the architecture.

3. Track more than readiness

Programs are killed by money and time, not just technical maturity. Add these as first-class, source-backed fields.

Dimension What it captures
Budget / funding Appropriations, contract value, overruns. NASA OIG and GAO sources already contain much of this.
Schedule slip Delta from the original baseline date and the trend over time. Events hint at slips but it is not a metric.
Risk register Explicit likelihood × severity, kept separate from the readiness status.
Provider / contractor Who is building each capability and the contract type — fixed-price vs. cost-plus matters a lot here.
Quantitative targets kWe of surface power, kg of cargo to the surface, crew-days of habitation, number of crew.

Suggested sequencing

Do not boil the ocean. Scope as one feature branch per step, in priority order.

  1. Fill the surface-systems capability gaps (life support, radiation, dust, night survival, water prospecting). Cheap — same schema, more curated records — and it makes "lunar base" honest rather than "Artemis transport." It also makes the Sustained-Presence Index mean what it claims.
  2. Add the China ILRS and CLPS tracks. Biggest visible payoff for milestone and timeline density, and it reframes the dashboard as a lunar-base race rather than one agency's status page.
  3. Add the budget and schedule-slip dimensions once the entity set is richer, then layer in the risk register and provider fields.
